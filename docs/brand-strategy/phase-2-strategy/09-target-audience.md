# 09 — Target Audience

## 1. Audience Overview

ALLONE exists at a specific inflection point in the market: AI has moved from experimental to essential, but the vast majority of businesses — particularly outside Silicon Valley — have no clear path to adopting it. The consultancies that understand AI charge enterprise rates. The freelancers who charge less lack the infrastructure to deliver reliably. ALLONE sits in the gap, offering production-grade AI automation at a price point that mid-market and growth-stage companies can absorb without board approval.

Our audience is defined not solely by demographics or firmographics, but by a shared condition: they know AI will reshape their industry, they cannot afford to wait, and they cannot afford the incumbents. They are operationally mature enough to have processes worth automating, yet lean enough that every dollar of efficiency gain hits the bottom line directly.

ALLONE's dual-geography model — rooted in Tbilisi with a commercial presence in Belgium — allows us to serve two distinct but complementary markets. The Georgian market provides depth, reputation, and reference clients. The European market provides scale, higher contract values, and global credibility. Both markets share the same fundamental need: accessible, high-quality AI implementation without the overhead of traditional consulting.

With five paying clients acquired through zero outbound sales effort, 30 commission-based salespeople now deployed, and a delivery cost structure that undercuts competitors by up to 90%, the question is not whether demand exists — it is how to focus that demand into the highest-value segments.

This document defines who those segments are, what drives their decisions, and how ALLONE should prioritize its attention across them.

## 2. Primary Audience: Georgian SMBs & Mid-Market

### Firmographic Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Company Size** | 10–200 employees |
| **Annual Revenue** | $500K–$10M |
| **Geography** | Tbilisi, Batumi, and secondary Georgian cities |
| **Organizational Maturity** | Established operations, some digital presence, limited or no AI adoption |
| **Tech Infrastructure** | Basic CRM or ERP, spreadsheet-heavy workflows, social media marketing |

### Target Industries

- **Retail & E-commerce** — Inventory management, customer service automation, personalized recommendations. Georgian retail is digitizing rapidly; chains like Nikora, Goodwill, and Europroduct are investing in tech but lack AI partners.
- **Hospitality & Tourism** — Chatbots for booking, multilingual guest communication, review management, dynamic pricing. Georgia's tourism sector (8M+ visitors pre-pandemic) runs largely on manual coordination.
- **Financial Services** — Loan processing automation, fraud detection, customer onboarding, document processing. Microfinance institutions and insurance companies are prime candidates.
- **Real Estate** — Lead qualification bots, automated property matching, document generation, market analysis tools. The Tbilisi real estate market is active but operationally primitive.
- **Healthcare** — Appointment scheduling, patient intake automation, medical record digitization, insurance claim processing.
- **Education** — Student enrollment automation, adaptive learning tools, administrative workflow optimization, AI tutoring assistants.

### Decision Makers

The primary decision maker in Georgian SMBs is almost always the **CEO or founder**. Georgian business culture is centralized — the person who signs the check is usually the person who evaluates the pitch. Secondary influencers include **operations managers** and, in larger mid-market firms, **finance directors** who control discretionary budgets.

### Pain Points

1. **Manual process overload** — Staff spend 40–60% of time on repetitive tasks that could be automated. Data entry, customer inquiries, scheduling, and reporting consume disproportionate resources.
2. **High agency costs with low accountability** — Previous experiences with traditional agencies often left a bad taste: overpriced websites, delayed timelines, generic solutions. There is active distrust of the agency model.
3. **Limited internal tech expertise** — Most Georgian SMBs do not have a CTO or technical lead. Technology decisions are made by generalists who need partners, not vendors.
4. **Competitive pressure** — As larger Georgian companies adopt AI (TBC, BOG, Wissol), smaller firms feel the pressure to modernize but lack a clear starting point.
5. **Language and localization** — Many international AI tools do not support Georgian language, creating a gap that local providers can fill.

### Budget Parameters

| Project Type | Typical Budget Range |
|---|---|
| AI Chatbot (single-channel) | $2,000–$5,000 |
| Workflow Automation | $3,000–$8,000 |
| Custom AI Solution | $8,000–$15,000 |
| Website + AI Integration | $5,000–$12,000 |
| Consulting Engagement | $2,000–$6,000 |

Average contract value target: **$5,000–$8,000**. Upsell potential within 12 months: 2–3x initial engagement through additional modules, maintenance, and expanded automation scope.

## 3. Secondary Audience: Belgian & EU SMBs

### Firmographic Profile

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Company Size** | 20–500 employees |
| **Annual Revenue** | $1M–$50M |
| **Geography** | Belgium (primary), Netherlands, Germany, France (secondary) |
| **Organizational Maturity** | Established operations, existing digital infrastructure, early-stage AI exploration |
| **Tech Infrastructure** | CRM (Salesforce, HubSpot), ERP (SAP, Odoo), marketing automation tools |

### Target Industries

- **Manufacturing** — Predictive maintenance, quality control automation, supply chain optimization, production scheduling. Belgian manufacturing is strong but faces labor cost pressure that AI directly addresses.
- **Logistics & Supply Chain** — Route optimization, warehouse automation, demand forecasting, customs documentation processing. Belgium's position as an EU logistics hub creates natural demand.
- **Professional Services** — Law firms, accounting firms, and consultancies seeking document automation, client intake bots, knowledge management systems, and billing optimization.
- **E-commerce** — Customer service automation, product recommendation engines, inventory forecasting, personalized marketing. The Benelux e-commerce market exceeds $30B annually.

### Decision Makers

European SMBs have more distributed decision-making than Georgian counterparts. The primary contact is typically the **CTO or Head of IT**, but budget approval involves the **COO** or **CFO**. In companies undergoing digital transformation, a dedicated **Digital Transformation Lead** may own the relationship. Marketing directors increasingly influence AI purchasing decisions for customer-facing automation.

### Pain Points

1. **Big 4 pricing without Big 4 budgets** — Accenture, Deloitte, and McKinsey dominate enterprise AI consulting in Europe. SMBs are priced out but have the same needs at smaller scale.
2. **AI pilot fatigue** — Many have run proof-of-concept projects that never reached production. They want implementation partners, not researchers.
3. **Talent scarcity** — Hiring AI engineers in Western Europe is prohibitively expensive ($120K–$180K+ per year). Outsourcing to a specialized agency is often the rational choice.
4. **Regulatory compliance** — GDPR, AI Act, and industry-specific regulations create complexity. Partners who understand compliance constraints are preferred.
5. **Integration complexity** — Existing tech stacks are more sophisticated, requiring AI solutions that integrate cleanly with Salesforce, SAP, Odoo, and other platforms already in use.

### Budget Parameters

| Project Type | Typical Budget Range |
|---|---|
| AI Chatbot (multi-channel, multilingual) | $5,000–$15,000 |
| Workflow Automation | $8,000–$25,000 |
| Custom AI Solution | $15,000–$50,000 |
| Consulting + Implementation | $10,000–$30,000 |

Average contract value target: **$15,000–$25,000**. The higher price point reflects greater solution complexity, multilingual requirements, and compliance overhead. Recurring revenue potential through maintenance and expansion contracts is significant.

## 4. Tertiary Audience: Regional Enterprises

### Profile

Georgian and regional enterprises — the tier that includes banks (TBC, Bank of Georgia), telecom operators (Magti, Silknet), large retail chains (SPAR Georgia, Wissol, Carrefour Georgia), utilities, and government-adjacent organizations. These are companies with 500+ employees, revenues exceeding $50M, and established IT departments.

### Why They Matter

ALLONE already operates in the orbit of this tier through its partnership with a top Georgian IT company whose portfolio includes TBC, SPAR, and Wissol clients. Enterprise contracts provide outsized revenue, brand credibility, and case studies that accelerate mid-market sales. A single enterprise engagement can equal 10–20 SMB contracts in value.

### Engagement Characteristics

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Decision Cycle** | 3–12 months. Committee-based approval involving IT, operations, finance, and executive leadership. |
| **Procurement Process** | Formal RFP or vendor evaluation. Requires references, security audits, and compliance documentation. |
| **Contract Structure** | Phased delivery: discovery, pilot, production rollout. Master service agreements with SOWs per phase. |
| **Budget Range** | $50,000–$200,000+ per engagement, with potential for multi-year contracts exceeding $500K. |
| **Key Relationships** | CTO, CIO, VP of Operations, Head of Digital. Executive sponsors required for budget release. |

### Strategic Approach

Enterprise deals are not pursued through the same channels as SMB acquisition. They require direct relationship selling, industry events, and strategic introductions — which ALLONE's existing partner network facilitates. The 30-person sales force is not optimized for enterprise; these deals come through the founding team's direct network and partner referrals.

## 5. Aspirational Audience: Global Tech-Forward Companies

### Profile

Startups and scale-ups worldwide — typically Series A through Series C — that need AI development capacity but cannot justify Bay Area or London engineering costs. These are companies with 15–100 employees, strong technical leadership, existing products, and a specific AI feature or capability they need built.

### The Nearshoring Proposition

Georgia is emerging as a nearshoring destination with a compelling value proposition: timezone overlap with Europe and the Middle East, English-proficient technical talent, competitive rates (60–80% lower than Western Europe), and a growing tech ecosystem. ALLONE's AI specialization makes it more attractive than generic outsourcing firms.

### Engagement Characteristics

| Attribute | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Decision Maker** | CTO or VP of Engineering |
| **Need** | Specific AI/ML feature development, model fine-tuning, data pipeline construction, AI integration into existing products |
| **Budget** | $10,000–$100,000 per project, with potential for ongoing retainer relationships |
| **Acquisition Channel** | Clutch, Toptal, direct referrals, GitHub presence, content marketing, conference visibility |
| **Timeline** | Short-cycle decisions (2–6 weeks). Technical evaluation based on portfolio and proof-of-capability. |

This audience is aspirational in the sense that it requires ALLONE to build international brand recognition and a portfolio of English-language case studies. It represents the long-term growth vector — the path from regional agency to global AI services firm.

## 7. Audience Prioritization Matrix

| Audience Segment | Revenue Potential | Acquisition Ease | Sales Cycle | Strategic Value | **Priority Rank** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Georgian SMBs & Mid-Market** | Medium ($5K–$15K) | High — local network, referrals, commission sales force | 2–6 weeks | High — builds portfolio, generates case studies, cash flow engine | **1 — Foundation** |
| **Belgian/EU SMBs** | High ($15K–$50K) | Medium — requires Belgium office activation, content marketing, Clutch presence | 4–12 weeks | Very High — validates international model, higher margins | **2 — Growth Engine** |
| **Regional Enterprises** | Very High ($50K–$200K+) | Low — long cycles, committee decisions, requires established credibility | 3–12 months | Very High — anchor clients, brand credibility, reference accounts | **3 — Credibility Anchor** |
| **Global Tech-Forward** | High ($10K–$100K) | Low-Medium — requires international brand building, portfolio depth | 2–6 weeks (once discovered) | High — diversifies geography, builds global reputation | **4 — Long-Term Vector** |

### Prioritization Logic

**Phase 1 (Now–Month 6):** Concentrate 70% of sales effort on Georgian SMBs. This is the fastest path to revenue, reference clients, and operational learning. The 30-person commission sales force is optimized for this segment. Every engagement should produce a case study.

**Phase 2 (Month 3–12):** Activate the Belgium office for EU SMB acquisition. Allocate 20% of effort here, increasing to 40% as the pipeline develops. Higher contract values justify longer sales cycles and marketing investment.

**Phase 3 (Month 6–18):** Pursue 2–3 enterprise engagements through partner introductions. These are relationship-driven, not volume-driven. One successful enterprise deployment generates more credibility than twenty SMB projects.

**Phase 4 (Month 12+):** Begin global outreach through content marketing, Clutch optimization, and conference presence. This audience compounds over time and requires patience.

## 8. Anti-Audience

Equally important as defining who ALLONE serves is defining who it does not. Pursuing the wrong clients drains resources, damages morale, and dilutes brand positioning. The following profiles represent deliberate exclusions.

### Budget-Only Shoppers

Companies whose sole evaluation criterion is price. They will always find someone cheaper on Fiverr or Upwork. They do not value expertise, they do not become reference clients, and they consume disproportionate support resources while negotiating every invoice. If the first question is "can you do it for half that price?" and there is no discussion of outcomes, walk away.

### Enterprise RFP Mills

Large organizations that issue RFPs to dozens of vendors with no genuine intent to select a smaller firm. The RFP process exists to benchmark their preferred vendor's pricing or to satisfy procurement policy. Responding to blind RFPs where ALLONE has no existing relationship and no insider context is a negative-ROI activity. Enterprise deals should only be pursued when a warm introduction or existing relationship provides a genuine path to selection.

### "Build Me an AI" Without a Problem

Prospects who want AI because it is trendy but cannot articulate what business problem they are solving. These engagements inevitably end in scope creep, shifting requirements, and dissatisfaction — because there was never a clear success criterion. ALLONE should qualify every lead against a specific, measurable pain point before entering proposal stage.

### One-Person Operations and Pre-Revenue Startups

Solo entrepreneurs and pre-revenue startups rarely have the budget, operational complexity, or process maturity to benefit from ALLONE's services. Their needs are better served by no-code tools (Zapier, Make, Chatbase) or templates. Engaging them consumes sales and delivery time that should be directed toward clients who can afford proper implementation and have processes worth automating.

### Clients Seeking Staff Augmentation Only

Companies that want to rent individual developers by the hour, with no project scope or outcome defined. ALLONE is a solutions partner, not a body shop. Staff augmentation commoditizes the team, eliminates margin, and prevents ALLONE from delivering the integrated AI solutions that define its value proposition.

### Geographies Without Strategic Value

At this stage, pursuing clients in markets where ALLONE has no presence, no network, and no competitive advantage (e.g., Southeast Asia, Latin America) is a distraction. Geographic expansion should follow the Belgium model: a specific partner, a specific market entry strategy, and a specific revenue target. Opportunistic global deals are acceptable; systematic pursuit of unfocused geographies is not.
