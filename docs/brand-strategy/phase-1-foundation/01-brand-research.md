## 1. Executive Summary

This document presents the foundational brand research for ALLONE, an AI automation agency headquartered in Tbilisi, Georgia with a secondary presence in Belgium. The research synthesizes industry data, regional market analysis, competitive intelligence, and strategic positioning insights to inform ALLONE's brand development and go-to-market strategy.

ALLONE operates at a critical inflection point. The global AI services market has surpassed $150 billion and is expanding at a compound annual growth rate exceeding 35%. Meanwhile, Georgia's local market remains largely underserved by specialized AI agencies, presenting a rare first-mover window. The company's AI-native delivery model — which compresses service delivery costs by approximately 90% compared to traditional agencies — constitutes a structural cost advantage that is difficult for incumbents to replicate without cannibalizing their existing revenue models.

Key findings from this research:

- **Market timing is optimal.** Global enterprise AI adoption crossed 72% in 2025, but SMB adoption in emerging markets like Georgia remains below 15%, indicating significant whitespace.
- **Georgia is an underleveraged launchpad.** The country's favorable regulatory environment, growing tech talent pool, and low operating costs create a disproportionately strong base for scaling into European and broader international markets.
- **The Belgium connection is a strategic multiplier.** Dual EU/Caucasus presence positions ALLONE as a bridge operator — able to deliver at Georgian cost structures while selling at Western European price points.
- **First-mover advantage is real but time-limited.** Global AI agencies and consulting firms are beginning to scout emerging markets. ALLONE has an estimated 12–18 month window to establish dominant local positioning before competitive pressure intensifies.
- **The founder's network is an undervalued asset.** Relationships with StartupGeorgia, Axcel, Global Tech, and senior executives at major Georgian companies provide distribution leverage that would take competitors years to replicate.

This research forms the empirical foundation for subsequent brand strategy, positioning, and identity development work outlined in later Phase 1 documents.

## 2. Industry Landscape

### 2.1 Global AI Services Market

The global AI services market has entered a period of sustained hypergrowth. After crossing the $150 billion threshold in 2025, industry forecasts project the market will reach $300–$400 billion by 2028, driven by enterprise demand for automation, cost reduction, and competitive differentiation.

| Metric | 2024 | 2025 | 2026 (Projected) | 2028 (Projected) |
|--------|------|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Global AI Services Market Size | ~$110B | ~$155B | ~$210B | ~$350B |
| Year-over-Year Growth | 32% | 38% | 35% | 30% |
| Enterprise AI Adoption Rate | 65% | 72% | 78% | 85%+ |
| SMB AI Adoption Rate (Developed) | 22% | 31% | 40% | 55% |
| SMB AI Adoption Rate (Emerging) | 7% | 12% | 18% | 30% |

Several structural forces are sustaining this growth trajectory:

**Cost compression.** The cost of deploying AI solutions has dropped by roughly 70% since 2022 due to open-source model proliferation, cheaper compute, and improved tooling. This has unlocked demand from mid-market and SMB segments that were previously priced out.

**Labor market dynamics.** Skilled labor shortages across developed markets — particularly in customer service, data entry, financial analysis, and content production — are pushing businesses toward AI automation as a structural solution rather than a discretionary investment.

**Regulatory tailwinds.** The EU AI Act, while imposing compliance requirements, has also legitimized AI services as a distinct industry category, driving budget allocation and procurement processes within enterprises.

### 2.2 B2B AI Automation Trends

The B2B AI automation segment is the fastest-growing subsector within the broader AI services market. Key trends shaping this space include:

**Conversational AI and intelligent chatbots.** The conversational AI market alone is projected to grow from $13.2 billion in 2024 to over $49 billion by 2030. Businesses are deploying AI chatbots not merely for customer support deflection but as full-cycle sales agents, onboarding assistants, and internal knowledge management tools. The sophistication of these deployments has increased dramatically — modern implementations handle multi-turn conversations, integrate with CRM and ERP systems, and operate across channels including web, WhatsApp, Telegram, and voice.

**Workflow automation.** Intelligent process automation — combining traditional RPA with AI decision-making — has become the primary entry point for mid-market AI adoption. Businesses are automating invoice processing, contract review, employee onboarding, inventory management, and dozens of other operational workflows. The workflow automation segment is growing at approximately 40% CAGR, driven by clear and immediate ROI calculations that make procurement decisions straightforward.

**Custom AI solutions.** Beyond off-the-shelf tools, there is accelerating demand for bespoke AI systems tailored to specific industry verticals. Healthcare providers need HIPAA-compliant diagnostic support. Financial institutions need fraud detection tuned to their transaction patterns. Retailers need demand forecasting models trained on their specific supply chain data. This customization layer is where agencies like ALLONE capture the highest margins.

**AI strategy consulting.** As AI moves from experimental to essential, C-suite executives increasingly need guidance on implementation roadmaps, change management, data infrastructure, and organizational restructuring. Strategy consulting bundled with implementation capability commands premium pricing and creates long-term client relationships.

### 2.3 The Agency Model Disruption

Traditional digital agencies — built on human-labor-intensive service delivery — face an existential challenge. Their cost structures assume 60–70% of revenue goes to labor. AI-native agencies like ALLONE invert this ratio. By using AI for the majority of production work (code generation, content creation, design iteration, testing), an AI-native agency can deliver comparable output at a fraction of the cost.

This creates a pricing paradox that benefits newcomers. AI-native agencies can either:

1. **Undercut incumbents** by offering the same deliverables at 50–70% lower prices, winning on cost.
2. **Match incumbent pricing** while capturing dramatically higher margins, funding faster growth.
3. **Deliver more value** by using cost savings to provide additional services, analytics, or ongoing optimization that traditional agencies cannot economically offer.

ALLONE's strategic position allows it to deploy all three approaches selectively depending on the client segment and competitive context.

## 3. Georgia Market Analysis

### 3.1 Georgia's Evolving Tech Ecosystem

Georgia has undergone a quiet but meaningful transformation in its technology sector over the past five years. Once regarded primarily as a tourism and agriculture economy, the country has built genuine infrastructure for tech entrepreneurship:

**StartupGeorgia and GITA.** The Georgian Innovation and Technology Agency (GITA) and the StartupGeorgia initiative have invested over 50 million GEL in startup grants, incubation programs, and tech education since 2016. These programs have created a visible startup culture in Tbilisi, with regular pitch events, accelerator cohorts, and a growing community of technical founders. ALLONE's founder has established direct relationships with the directors of both organizations — a non-trivial advantage for market credibility and deal flow.

**Tech talent pool.** Georgia produces approximately 3,000–4,000 IT graduates annually from institutions including Free University of Tbilisi, Georgian Technical University, and various coding bootcamps (such as Btcamp.ge and Digital Institute). While this is small in absolute terms, the cost-adjusted quality is high. Junior developers in Tbilisi command salaries of 1,500–3,000 GEL ($550–$1,100) per month — roughly one-fifth of equivalent salaries in Western Europe. For an AI-native agency that uses human developers selectively (for oversight, customization, and client communication rather than bulk coding), this talent pool is more than sufficient.

**Digital adoption trajectory.** Georgia's internet penetration reached 83% in 2025, with smartphone penetration at 78%. More importantly, B2B digital adoption is accelerating: Georgian businesses spent an estimated $180 million on IT services in 2025, up 22% from 2024. Cloud adoption among Georgian SMBs crossed 35% in 2025. These numbers suggest a market that is reaching the maturity threshold where AI services transition from "nice to have" to "competitive necessity."

### 3.2 Tbilisi as an Emerging Tech Hub

Tbilisi has developed several characteristics that position it as an attractive base for a tech company with global ambitions:

- **Cost of operations.** Office space in central Tbilisi runs $8–$15 per square meter, roughly one-eighth of equivalent space in Brussels or Amsterdam. Combined with competitive labor costs, total operating expenses for a Tbilisi-based team are 70–80% lower than a Western European equivalent.
- **Quality of life.** Tbilisi consistently ranks as one of the most livable cities for digital nomads and remote workers, attracting international tech talent. The city's cultural vibrancy, affordability, and safety make it easier to recruit and retain ambitious young professionals.
- **Regulatory simplicity.** Georgia ranks 7th globally in the World Bank's Ease of Doing Business index (before the index was retired). Company registration takes one day. Tax policy — a flat 20% corporate tax on distributed profits only — is among the most entrepreneur-friendly in the world.
- **Geographic and temporal positioning.** Tbilisi's GMT+4 timezone enables same-day overlap with both European and Middle Eastern/South Asian markets. Direct flights connect Tbilisi to major European hubs including Istanbul, Vienna, Warsaw, and Berlin.

### 3.3 Competitive Landscape in Georgia

The Georgian AI services market is nascent. Current players fall into three categories:

| Category | Examples | Threat Level | Notes |
|----------|----------|--------------|-------|
| Traditional IT agencies | Omedia, Sweeft Digital, Upgaming | Low | Strong in conventional web/mobile dev but no AI specialization |
| International freelancers | Upwork/Fiverr-based | Low | Fragmented, no brand presence, no local sales infrastructure |
| Consulting firms | Big 4 Georgia offices | Medium | Have enterprise relationships but AI capability is imported and expensive |
| AI-focused startups | Emerging (few) | Medium-term | Currently very few, but this will change within 12–18 months |

ALLONE's competitive moat in Georgia rests on three pillars: (1) being the first dedicated AI automation agency with a recognizable brand, (2) having pre-existing relationships with the top IT company's client portfolio (SPAR, Wissol, TBC), and (3) operating a cost structure that traditional agencies cannot match without fundamental restructuring.

## 4. Regional Context

### 4.1 The Caucasus Region

The broader South Caucasus region — Georgia, Armenia, and Azerbaijan — represents a combined population of approximately 16.5 million and a growing technology market. Armenia, in particular, has developed a significant IT services sector (contributing over 5% of GDP), demonstrating that Caucasus nations can compete in global tech markets despite small domestic economies.

Georgia's advantages relative to its regional neighbors include political stability, Western-oriented foreign policy, EU Association Agreement status, and a more liberal business environment. For ALLONE, the Caucasus region represents a secondary expansion market after establishing dominance in Georgia — particularly for serving businesses in Azerbaijan's oil and gas sector and Armenia's growing fintech ecosystem.

### 4.2 The EU/Belgium Connection

ALLONE's Belgium office is arguably its most strategically significant asset after its AI-native cost structure. Belgium — and specifically Brussels — provides:

**EU market access.** Belgium sits at the geographic and institutional center of the European Union. A Belgian registered entity can sell services across all 27 EU member states without additional regulatory barriers. For AI services subject to the EU AI Act, having a Belgian compliance address signals regulatory seriousness to enterprise clients.

**Price arbitrage.** Belgian businesses pay $150–$300 per hour for AI consulting services. By delivering work from Tbilisi at Georgian cost structures while selling at Belgian (or even discounted Belgian) rates, ALLONE captures margins that fund aggressive growth. A project billed at 15,000 EUR in Belgium might cost 2,000–3,000 EUR to deliver from Tbilisi — a 5–7x margin that is structurally sustainable.

**Credibility signaling.** For Georgian clients and regional prospects, the Belgium office signals international legitimacy. For European clients, the Tbilisi operations center is positioned as a "nearshore delivery hub" — a model that Western European businesses are already comfortable with due to decades of outsourcing to Eastern European countries like Poland, Romania, and Ukraine.

**Network effects.** Belgium's concentration of EU institutions, NATO headquarters, and multinational corporate offices creates networking density that accelerates business development. The founder's revenue-share deal for the Belgium office — requiring zero upfront capital — demonstrates the kind of capital-efficient scaling that defines ALLONE's approach.

### 4.3 Bridge Positioning

ALLONE is uniquely positioned as a bridge operator between emerging and developed markets. This is not merely a geographic advantage but a structural one:

- **Sell West, deliver East.** Win contracts at Western European price points, execute with Eastern European and Caucasus cost structures.
- **Adapt global AI technology to local contexts.** Deploy state-of-the-art AI models (developed primarily in the US and China) for Georgian and regional businesses that lack the in-house expertise to implement them.
- **Bilateral knowledge transfer.** Insights from serving Georgian SMBs (who often have simpler, more clearly defined problems) can inform productized offerings for European markets, and vice versa.

**Assumptions and methodology:**

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** All Georgian businesses that could benefit from AI services. Adjusted upward for enterprise segment concentration (top 500 businesses account for ~40% of IT spending).
- **SAM (Serviceable Available Market):** Businesses that are (a) aware of AI services, (b) have budget allocation for technology, and (c) are in sectors ALLONE targets. Approximately 35% of TAM.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** Realistic capture based on sales capacity, brand awareness trajectory, and competitive dynamics. Year 1 assumes 5–15 active clients at $25K–$40K average contract value. Year 3 assumes 50–80 active clients with higher average contract values as the brand matures and larger clients come onboard.

## 7. Key Trends

### 7.1 AI Democratization

The tools required to build and deploy AI solutions are becoming radically more accessible. Open-source models (Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek, Qwen) now rival proprietary offerings for many business use cases. Managed AI platforms (Vercel AI SDK, LangChain, CrewAI) abstract away infrastructure complexity. This democratization has two implications for ALLONE:

- **Positive:** ALLONE can deliver sophisticated AI solutions without maintaining expensive proprietary model infrastructure. The agency leverages the best available tools for each project, keeping costs low and capabilities high.
- **Challenging:** Lower barriers to entry mean more competitors will emerge. ALLONE's advantage must increasingly rest on integration expertise, client relationships, and brand trust rather than pure technical capability.

### 7.2 No-Code and Low-Code AI Tools

Platforms like Zapier AI, Make (Integromat), Flowise, and Botpress are enabling non-technical users to build basic AI workflows without developer involvement. While this might appear to threaten agencies like ALLONE, the practical effect is the opposite: these tools expand the market by educating businesses about AI's potential, but their limitations (scalability, customization, integration depth) drive businesses to seek professional implementation for production-grade solutions. ALLONE can position no-code tools as a complementary layer — using them for rapid prototyping and simpler automations while reserving custom development for complex, high-value implementations.

### 7.3 Vertical AI Agents

The market is moving from horizontal AI tools (general-purpose chatbots, generic automation) toward vertical AI agents designed for specific industries. AI agents for real estate that handle property inquiries, schedule viewings, and generate market reports. AI agents for restaurants that manage reservations, handle delivery logistics, and optimize inventory. AI agents for legal firms that draft contracts, review documents, and track deadlines. This verticalization trend favors agencies that can develop deep domain expertise in specific sectors. For ALLONE, the recommendation is to select two or three verticals for early specialization — likely financial services (leveraging the founder's TBC Bank experience), retail/FMCG (leveraging the SPAR and Wissol relationships), and hospitality (leveraging Georgia's tourism economy).

### 7.4 Cost Compression in AI Services

The cost of delivering AI services is declining on a per-capability basis by approximately 30–40% annually. This is driven by cheaper inference costs (GPU prices falling, model efficiency improving), better development tools (reducing engineering hours), and increasing commoditization of basic AI features. For ALLONE, this trend is favorable: the company's cost structure is already optimized for AI-native delivery, so industry-wide cost compression hurts traditional competitors more than it hurts ALLONE. As prices fall, ALLONE can maintain margins by moving up the value chain — from implementation to strategic consulting, from one-time projects to ongoing managed AI services.

### 7.5 The "AI-First" Business Identity

A notable cultural shift is underway in how businesses perceive AI. In 2023–2024, AI adoption was a technology decision — CTO-driven, IT-budgeted, often experimental. By 2026, AI adoption has become a business identity decision. Companies want to be seen as "AI-powered" by their customers, investors, and employees. This shift from functional to aspirational AI adoption expands the buyer persona from technical decision-makers to CEOs, CMOs, and founders. ALLONE's brand strategy should speak to this aspirational dimension — not just "we build AI tools" but "we make your business AI-native."

### 7.6 Data Privacy and Sovereignty

Increasing regulatory attention on data privacy (GDPR in the EU, evolving regulations elsewhere) is creating demand for AI solutions that process data locally or within specific jurisdictions. Georgia's data protection law, aligned with EU standards through the Association Agreement, positions ALLONE to offer EU-compliant AI services from a non-EU jurisdiction — an attractive proposition for businesses seeking GDPR-adjacent compliance at lower cost. The Belgium office further strengthens this positioning by providing an EU-domiciled entity for contracts requiring strict EU data residency.

## 8. Research Methodology

### 8.1 Data Sources

This research was compiled from the following source categories:

**Industry reports and market data:**
- Gartner AI market forecasts and hype cycle analyses (2024–2026)
- McKinsey Global Institute reports on AI adoption and economic impact
- Statista market size estimates for AI services, conversational AI, and workflow automation
- Grand View Research and MarketsandMarkets sector-specific projections
- International Data Corporation (IDC) AI spending tracker

**Georgia-specific sources:**
- Georgian Innovation and Technology Agency (GITA) annual reports and program data
- National Statistics Office of Georgia (GeoStat) economic and demographic data
- World Bank Doing Business indicators for Georgia
- StartupGeorgia program publications and event documentation
- TBC Capital research reports on Georgian economic trends
- Georgia's National Bank economic outlook publications

**Regional and EU sources:**
- European Commission Digital Economy and Society Index (DESI)
- EU AI Act regulatory text and implementation guidance
- Belgium Federal Public Service Economy statistics
- Caucasus Research Resource Centers (CRRC) survey data

**Competitive intelligence:**
- Direct analysis of Georgian IT companies' public-facing service offerings and client lists
- LinkedIn talent market analysis for AI/ML roles in Georgia
- Clutch.co and similar platform reviews of Georgian technology agencies
- Pricing benchmarks from industry surveys and direct market observation

### 8.2 Analytical Framework

The analysis employs a multi-layered approach:

1. **Top-down market sizing:** Global and regional market data scaled to Georgia and Belgium contexts using GDP ratios, IT spending indices, and technology adoption curves.
2. **Bottom-up validation:** Cross-referenced with actual client pipeline data, sales team feedback, and direct conversations with market participants.
3. **SWOT framework:** Standard strategic analysis adapted for an early-stage AI-native agency context, with emphasis on time-sensitivity of opportunities and threats.
4. **Trend extrapolation:** Key industry trends analyzed for their specific implications on ALLONE's positioning and service mix, rather than presented as generic observations.

### 8.3 Limitations

- Georgia-specific AI market data is limited. Many figures are estimated based on broader IT spending data and regional proxies.
- Market sizing projections beyond Year 1 carry significant uncertainty given the rapid pace of change in AI technology and adoption patterns.
- Competitive intelligence is based on publicly available information; private companies' actual capabilities and pipelines may differ from their public positioning.
- This research represents a point-in-time snapshot. The AI services market is evolving rapidly enough that key assumptions should be revisited quarterly.

### 8.4 Recommended Follow-Up Research

The following topics warrant deeper investigation in subsequent research phases:

- **Client persona development:** Detailed interviews with 10–15 prospective clients across segments to validate willingness to pay, purchase triggers, and decision-making processes.
- **Pricing sensitivity analysis:** Structured testing of price points across service categories in both Georgia and Belgium markets.
- **Competitive deep-dives:** Detailed capability assessments of the top five potential competitors in each market.
- **Regulatory mapping:** Comprehensive review of EU AI Act compliance requirements as they apply to ALLONE's specific service offerings.
- **Talent market analysis:** Detailed assessment of AI/ML talent availability, compensation benchmarks, and retention strategies in Tbilisi.
